Study Guide

I. States of Matter

A. Solids

  • Definition: Matter with a definite shape and volume.
  • Properties:
    • Particles are closely packed in a fixed arrangement.
    • Vibrate but do not move from place to place.
    • Incompressible and rigid.

B. Liquids

  • Definition: Matter that has a definite volume but takes the shape of its container.
  • Properties:
    • Particles are close together but can move/slide past one another.
    • Fluid and can flow.
    • Slightly compressible.

C. Gases

  • Definition: Matter without a definite shape or volume.
  • Properties:
    • Particles are far apart and move freely.
    • Fill any container they're in.
    • Highly compressible.

D. Plasma

  • Definition: Ionized gas with free electrons.
  • Properties:
    • Conducts electricity.
    • Affected by magnetic fields.
    • Found in stars and fluorescent lights.

II. Physical and Chemical Changes

A. Physical Changes

  • Definition: Changes that alter the form of a substance but not its chemical composition.
  • Examples:
    • Melting ice.
    • Tearing paper.
    • Dissolving sugar in water.
  • Characteristics:
    • Generally reversible.
    • No new substances formed.

B. Chemical Changes

  • Definition: Changes that result in the formation of new chemical substances.
  • Examples:
    • Burning wood.
    • Rusting iron.
    • Baking a cake.
  • Indicators:
    • Color change.
    • Gas production.
    • Formation of a precipitate.
    • Energy change (heat, light).

III. Classification of Matter by Composition

A. Pure Substances

  1. Elements
    • Simplest form of matter.
    • Cannot be broken down by chemical means.
    • Examples: Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Gold (Au).
  2. Compounds
    • Made of two or more elements chemically combined.
    • Fixed proportions.
    • Examples: Water (H₂O), Carbon Dioxide (CO₂).

B. Mixtures

  1. Homogeneous Mixtures (Solutions)
    • Uniform composition throughout.
    • Examples: Saltwater, air.
  2. Heterogeneous Mixtures
    • Non-uniform composition.
    • Examples: Salad, sand and water.

IV. Theories of Heat

A. Caloric Theory

  • Tenets:
    • Heat is a weightless fluid called caloric.
    • Flows from hotter to colder bodies.
  • Experimental Support:
    • Early observations of heat flow.
  • Why It's Wrong:
    • Couldn't explain heat generation via friction.
    • Disproved by experiments showing heat as energy transfer.
