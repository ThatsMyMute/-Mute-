I. States of Matter

  • Definition: Matter with a definite shape and volume.
  • Properties:
    • Particles are closely packed in a fixed arrangement.
    • Vibrate but do not move from place to place.
    • Incompressible and rigid.
  • Definition: Matter that has a definite volume but takes the shape of its container.
  • Properties:
    • Particles are close together but can move/slide past one another.
    • Fluid and can flow.
    • Slightly compressible.
  • Definition: Matter without a definite shape or volume.
  • Properties:
    • Particles are far apart and move freely.
    • Fill any container they're in.
    • Highly compressible.
  • Definition: Ionized gas with free electrons.
  • Properties:
    • Conducts electricity.
    • Affected by magnetic fields.
    • Found in stars and fluorescent lights.

II. Physical and Chemical Changes

  • Definition: Changes that alter the form of a substance but not its chemical composition.
  • Examples:
    • Melting ice.
    • Tearing paper.
    • Dissolving sugar in water.
  • Characteristics:
    • Generally reversible.
    • No new substances formed.
  • Definition: Changes that result in the formation of new chemical substances.
  • Examples:
    • Burning wood.
    • Rusting iron.
    • Baking a cake.
  • Indicators:
    • Color change.
    • Gas production.
    • Formation of a precipitate.
    • Energy change (heat, light).

III. Classification of Matter by Composition

  1. Elements
    • Simplest form of matter.
    • Cannot be broken down by chemical means.
    • Examples: Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Gold (Au).
  2. Compounds
    • Made of two or more elements chemically combined.
    • Fixed proportions.
    • Examples: Water (H₂O), Carbon Dioxide (CO₂).
  1. Homogeneous Mixtures (Solutions)
    • Uniform composition throughout.
    • Examples: Saltwater, air.
  2. Heterogeneous Mixtures
    • Non-uniform composition.
    • Examples: Salad, sand and water.

IV. Theories of Heat

  • Tenets:
    • Heat is a weightless fluid called caloric.
    • Flows from hotter to colder bodies.
  • Experimental Support:
    • Early observations of heat flow.
  • Why It's Wrong:
    • Couldn't explain heat generation via friction.
    • Disproved by experiments showing heat as energy transfer.
  • Tenets:
    • Heat is the kinetic energy of particles.
    • Temperature measures average kinetic energy.
  • Experimental Support:
    • Explains gas laws and heat capacities.
  • Advantages:
    • Accounts for molecular motion.
    • Explains heat transfer mechanisms.
  • Tenets:
    • Heat is energy transfer due to temperature difference.
    • Governed by laws of thermodynamics.
  • Experimental Support:
    • Broad applications in engines, refrigeration.
  • Improvements:
    • Introduces concepts of entropy and enthalpy.
    • More comprehensive understanding of energy systems.

VIII. Laws of Composition

  • Definition: A chemical compound always contains the same proportion of elements by mass.
  • Example: Water is always 11.19% hydrogen and 88.81% oxygen by mass.
  • Definition: When two elements form more than one compound, the mass ratios are simple whole numbers.
  • Example: CO and CO₂ have mass ratios of oxygen to carbon that are in a 1:2 ratio.

IX. Dalton's Atomic Model

  • Postulates:
    1. Matter consists of indivisible atoms.
    2. Atoms of the same element are identical.
    3. Compounds are combinations of different atoms.
    4. Chemical reactions involve rearrangement of atoms.
    5. Atoms are neither created nor destroyed.
  • Impact on Chemistry:
    • Provided a framework for understanding chemical reactions.
    • Explained conservation of mass.
  • Limitations:
    • Atoms are divisible into subatomic particles.
    • Existence of isotopes contradicts identical atoms postulate.
    • Modern atomic theory incorporates quantum mechanics.
